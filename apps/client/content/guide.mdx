There are essentially two modes of AI interaction, which we might call "GPS Mode" versus "Tour Guide Mode":

**GPS Mode**:

- You need to know your destination clearly
- The clearer your end goal, the better the directions
- Example: "I need a formal email to decline a vendor's proposal while maintaining the possibility of future business"
- Success depends heavily on your ability to specify what "good" looks like
- Common tasks: Summarizing, translating, formatting, specific analysis

**Tour Guide Mode**:

- You're exploring possibilities
- You have a general direction but want to discover options
- Example: "I'm thinking about improving our customer onboarding process - what approaches should we consider?"
- Success comes from iterative dialogue and refinement
- Common tasks: Brainstorming, ideation, problem-solving, strategy discussions

The frustration often comes when people use "Tour Guide Mode" questions for "GPS Mode" tasks or vice versa. It's like telling a GPS "take me somewhere nice for lunch" - it needs more specifics to be helpful. Conversely, being too rigid when brainstorming can limit valuable creative insights.

# Chain of Thought

Chain of Thought: Breaking Down Complex Tasks
Think of AI like a smart colleague who works best when problems are broken down. There are two main approaches:

**Autonomous CoT**

- You provide a high-level goal
- AI explores and discovers on its own
- Example: "I want to improve our customer onboarding process"
- AI might suggest different approaches, analyze data, and propose solutions

**Technical Example**:
"Think step by step about optimizing this API's response time"

- AI examines current flow
- Spots bottlenecks
- Suggests performance improvements

**Guided CoT**

- You provide a high-level goal
- You guide the AI through the steps
- Example: "I want to improve our customer onboarding process"
- You might suggest steps like "First, analyze our current onboarding process"
- AI follows your guidance, making adjustments as needed

**Business Example**:
Developing new market entry strategy:

1. "First analyze competitor pricing in the region"
2. "Now identify gaps in their service offerings"
3. "Finally, propose our differentiation strategy"

**Technical Example**:
Refactoring legacy system:

1. "First map the current dependencies"
2. "Now identify modernization priorities"
3. "Finally, outline the migration steps"

Rather than the AI working alone, guided CoT is like having a skilled partner - whether you're crafting business strategy or architecting systems, you're directing while the AI handles the deep analysis.

Clearing the Cognitive Market: Getting the Most from AI Brainstorming
When brainstorming with AI, use this technique to understand how well you're exploring the possibility space:

First, ask for 10 ideas/solutions for your challenge
Then ask for 10 MORE, but specify "no overlaps with previous ideas"

What happens next tells you a lot:
If the AI easily generates 10 new, distinct ideas:

- You're in "blue ocean" territory
- Lots of unexplored possibilities remain
- Good for: Innovation, creative projects, finding novel approaches
- Example: "Give me 10 new ways to improve remote team engagement" (then 10 more)

If the AI struggles or shows overlap:

- You've "cleared the market" of obvious solutions
- The problem space is well-defined
- Good for: Tactical problems, optimization challenges
- Example: "Give me 10 ways to reduce meeting length" (overlaps likely in second set)

Pro Tip: This technique helps you understand if you need to:

- Explore more broadly (if too constrained)
- Focus more narrowly (if too scattered)
- Shift perspective entirely (if stuck)

📝 Remember: Sometimes hitting the edges quickly is good - it means you've mapped the key solution space. Other times, finding endless possibilities means you need more constraints.
